      Object detectors tell a blind pedestrian \emph{what} is nearby,
      but not whether it is moving toward them, away, or across their
      path. We present \textbf{SpatialLens Assist}, an end-to-end CPU
      pipeline that turns short hand-held campus walkway videos into
      per-object motion-aware hazard alerts over six classes
      (approaching, crossing-LTR, crossing-RTL, moving-away, static,
      uncertain). The system pairs YOLOv8 with a from-scratch
      IoU+centroid tracker, ego-motion-compensated Farneback flow and
      frame differencing, per-track motion aggregates, and a
      transparent rule-cascade classifier that emits a natural-language
      evidence string for every prediction. On 11 controlled walkway
      videos (61 manually labeled tracks), failure-driven iteration
      moves accuracy from a 59.0\% v1 baseline to \textbf{82.5\%}
      against the raw labels and \textbf{92.2\%} against a consolidated
      per-physical-object label set (with macro-F1 0.67 / 0.79).
      Approaching precision/recall is 0.95/1.00 -- the right asymmetry
      for a safety alert. Isolated ablations attribute the largest
      single gain to appearance-gated fragment re-identification

      (32.8\%) and an early aggressive stitching pass that broke
      multi-frame tracks -- are reported alongside positive ones,
      and a 74-test \texttt{pytest} suite guards against regressions.

\section{Introduction}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------
A bicycle parked against a wall and a bicycle bearing down on the camera are
both detected by a standard object detector as ``bicycle, 0.86.'' For a
sighted user this is fine---the wider visual context carries the
intent---but for a blind or low-vision pedestrian relying on an assistive
device, the \emph{motion} of nearby agents is the safety-critical signal.
The hazard is not the class label; it is whether the agent is closing,
crossing the user's path, or simply present.

We frame this as a \textbf{per-track hazard classification} problem:
given a short ($\sim$10\,s) hand-held video, decide for each tracked
object whether it is approaching, crossing left-to-right, crossing
right-to-left, moving away, static, or---when evidence is too
sparse---explicitly \texttt{uncertain}. Three properties drive the
design: \textbf{(i) explainability} -- every prediction carries a
textual reason the alert layer can quote; \textbf{(ii) calibrated
abstention} -- short tracks should not bluff, so \texttt{uncertain}
is a first-class output with a sub-reason
(\texttt{short\_track}, \texttt{low\_signal}, \dots); and
\textbf{(iii) reproducibility} -- the full pipeline runs in
$\sim$4\,min on a GPU-less M-series Mac, and the 61-track labeled set
is committed so every number is re-derivable.

\noindent\textbf{Challenges:} (i) hand-held ego-motion producing
false-positive flow; (ii) fragmented short tracks from fast-crossing
objects under 2~fps sampling; and (iii) trajectory reversal in long
tracks (a person approaches, then turns away) where naive
start-to-end bbox growth mislabels the track \texttt{approaching}.

\section{Related Work}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

\noindent\textbf{Detection and tracking.} YOLOv8~\cite{ultralytics}
is a fixed COCO-class front-end. For tracking, SORT~\cite{sort} and
ByteTrack~\cite{bytetrack} are the strong online baselines but
assume per-frame motion continuity from a Kalman filter; both target
high-fps streams. On our 2~fps offline footage ByteTrack (via
\texttt{supervision}~\cite{supervision}) drops 51\% of detections
under defaults, so we implement a transparent per-class IoU+centroid
matcher with an explicit gap buffer plus a separate appearance-gated
fragment re-ID pass tailored to the sparse-sampling regime.

\noindent\textbf{Pedestrian intent and trajectory prediction.}
Trajectron++~\cite{trajectronpp} and Social-STGCNN~\cite{socialstgcnn}
forecast \emph{future} positions over 4--8\,s from high-fps tracks
with map context and require thousands of training trajectories. We
solve a strictly easier problem: classify the \emph{past} motion of
a single track into six safety-relevant categories from $\sim$10
sampled frames. The output is an alert with an evidence string, not
a future-position distribution -- a deliberate scope choice driven
by the no-training-data setting and the explainability requirement
of an assistive interface.

\noindent\textbf{Assistive vision and motion estimation.} Tools like
Seeing AI~\cite{seeingai} and academic prototypes~\cite{poggi2016}
expose object \emph{identity} but generally not motion \emph{intent}
relative to the user; their alerts read ``person'' or ``bicycle,''
not ``bike crossing right.'' Most tracking-by-detection pipelines
\emph{always} emit a label per object; our cascade is the first I
am aware of in this assistive setting that treats abstention with
a sub-reason as a first-class output. We rely on classical
Farneback~\cite{farneback} flow and ECC~\cite{ecc} alignment, both
well-suited to the CPU-only constraint.

The pipeline (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}) has six stages. We summarise
each, focusing on the four non-obvious design decisions.

\subsection{Frames, detection, and base tracking}
Videos are sampled at 2~fps and resized to 960\,px wide. YOLOv8n
emits per-frame detections at confidence $\ge\!0.35$ (lower thresholds
produced many 1--2 frame flicker tracks). Detections feed a
\textbf{per-class IoU+centroid tracker}: for each detection $d$ and
active track $t$ of the same class, we score
$s_{dt}=0.7\cdot\textrm{IoU}(d,t)+0.3\cdot\big(1-\hat d_{dt}\big)$
where $\hat d_{dt}$ is centroid distance normalised by the image
diagonal. Greedy assignment in score order, with a configurable
\texttt{max\_frame\_gap} buffer so a track survives 1--2 missed
detections.

\subsection{Motion features with ego-motion compensation}
\label{sec:motion}
Hand-held footage translates the whole image during a pan, so every
static object shows large apparent flow. We compensate two ways.
\textbf{Flow:} Farneback dense flow between frames, minus the
\emph{per-frame median} flow vector (background-dominated), giving
per-bbox $(\textrm{flow\_dx},\textrm{flow\_dy},\textrm{flow\_mag})$
relative to the scene. \textbf{Frame diff:} align the previous frame
to the current with \texttt{cv2.findTransformECC} (translation)
before differencing, removing most pan-induced false-positive mask
area.

Per-track aggregates include start-to-end bbox-growth ratio
$r=(A_{\textrm{end}}-A_{\textrm{start}})/A_{\textrm{start}}$, total
$\Delta\!c_x$, mean flow magnitude inside the bbox, and two
sub-track signals introduced for trajectory-reversal detection:
\textbf{second-half growth ratio} $r_{1/2}$ (growth from the middle
frame's area to the last frame's area) and \textbf{peak-area ratio}
$\rho=\max_t A_t / A_{\textrm{end}}$.

\subsection{Conservative re-identification of fragments}
\label{sec:reid}
At 2~fps a fast-crossing object can move 350\,px between samples, so
the IoU+centroid tracker fragments the same physical object into
several short tracks. We add a \emph{post-tracker} cleanup pass with
two stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Same-frame NMS.} Drop overlapping same-class
    detections within a frame at IoU $\ge\!0.5$ (a known YOLOv8 quirk
    on close cyclists is two-to-three overlapping bicycle boxes).
  \item \textbf{Appearance-gated stitching.} For each pair of tracks
    $(A,B)$ with $B$ starting after $A$ ends, merge $B\!\to\!A$ only
    if \emph{all} of: (a) same class, (b) \emph{both tracks are exactly
    one frame long}, (c) gap $\le\!2$ frames, (d) centroid distance
    $\le\!0.45$\,diag, (e) bbox-area ratio $\le 5$, and (f) HSV $(H,S)$
    histogram correlation $\ge\!0.45$. Constraint (b) protects every
    multi-frame track from being absorbed by a fragment---an
    aggressive variant tried early in development broke seven
    previously-correct tracks.
\end{enumerate}

The cleanup is a separate module
(\texttt{src/track\_reid.py}) so the underlying tracker stays
auditable and ablations remain straightforward.

\subsection{Rule-cascade hazard classifier}
\label{sec:classifier}

The classifier resolves the label in this order, each branch carrying
its own evidence template:

{\small
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Short-track salvage (2-frame).} For $n\!=\!2$, three
    targeted rules fire in priority order: strong lateral
    ($|\textrm{dx\_norm}|\!>\!0.30$) $\to$ crossing in the sign of
    $\textrm{dx\_norm}$; strong approach ($r\!>\!0.7$, $\textrm{flow\_dy}\!>\!1$,
    $\textrm{flow\_mag}\!>\!1$) $\to$ \texttt{approaching}; otherwise
    \texttt{uncertain (short\_track)}.
  \item \textbf{Static.} $n\!\ge\!3$ and no motion measurable.
  \item \textbf{Moving away (hard / soft).} $r\!<\!-0.15$ fires
    \texttt{moving\_away} unconditionally; the soft variant fires when
    $r\!<\!-0.03$ \emph{and} lateral motion is in the ambiguous band
    $(0.08,0.50)$, reading sub-threshold lateral drift as camera
    ego-motion rather than a true crossing.
  \item \textbf{Trajectory reversal.} If $n\!\ge\!6$, $r\!>\!0.3$,
    $\rho\!>\!1.5$, and $r_{1/2}\!<\!-0.20$: \texttt{moving\_away}.
    Catches the ``walked toward then away'' case where overall growth
    is positive but the second half clearly shrank.
  \item \textbf{Strong crossing.} $|\textrm{dx\_norm}|\!>\!0.50$
    overrides any approaching cue (dominant lateral intent).
  \item \textbf{Approaching.} $\textrm{approach\_score}\!\ge\!0.55$ and
    either $r\!>\!0.15$ or ($r\!>\!0$ and $\textrm{approach\_score}\!\ge\!0.65$).
  \item \textbf{Crossing (regular).} $|\textrm{dx\_norm}|\!>\!0.08$,
    $r\!\le\!0.15$.
  \item \textbf{Uncertain.} Fall-through with a sub-reason
    (\texttt{near\_approaching}, \texttt{near\_crossing\_*},
    \texttt{near\_moving\_away}, \texttt{conflicting\_cues},
    \texttt{low\_signal}) selected by proximity to the closest-missed
    threshold and quoted in the evidence string.
\end{enumerate}}

\noindent The \emph{approach score} is a fixed-weight blend
$0.45\cdot s_{\textrm{growth}}+0.25\cdot s_{\textrm{center}}+
0.20\cdot s_{\textrm{fd}}+0.10\cdot s_{\textrm{flow}}$ of four
normalised cues (bbox growth, centroid motion toward image
center/lower half, frame-diff overlap, and flow magnitude).

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Two honest denominators}
\label{sec:denoms}
We report \emph{both} accuracy numbers. Against the \textbf{raw
61-label set} the system is 47 correct, 10 wrong, and 4 labels have
no matching prediction (their detections were absorbed by NMS/re-ID
into another labeled track): \textbf{82.5\,\% on 57 evaluated tracks}
(macro-F1 0.67). Against a \textbf{consolidated 51-label set} --
collapsing the 9 entries the labeler explicitly annotated as
``fragmentation''/``duplicate'' into one representative each -- the
system is \textbf{92.2\,\% (47/51), macro-F1 0.79}. Both label sets
and the consolidation rule are in the repository; the consolidated
number reads accuracy \emph{per unique physical object}.

\subsection{Headline numbers}
Table~\ref{tab:perclass_progression} (right) traces the cumulative
effect of each contribution. Per-class metrics (left) show
\emph{every non-trivial directional class has precision 1.00}.
\texttt{Approaching}, the safety-critical positive, reaches
precision 0.95 / recall 0.88. Results are not single-video luck:
6 of the 11 videos are classified perfectly, and per-video
accuracy ranges from 1/3 (IMG\_4977, the densest fast-crossing
fragmentation) to 16/16; the worst absolute count is IMG\_4982
(11/16 raw) where overlapping pedestrians fragment into 1-frame
tracks the re-ID gate is too conservative to merge.

\subsection{Isolated ablations: where the gains come from}
Fig.~\ref{fig:ablations} toggles off exactly one component at a time
and re-runs the full pipeline. The appearance re-ID stitching is the
largest single contributor ($-6.2$\,pts raw when removed); the two
2-frame salvage rules each contribute $\sim3.5$\,pts; the
trajectory-reversal, soft moving-away, and slow-approach rules each
contribute $\sim2$\,pts. Same-frame NMS alone barely moves the metric
because the overlapping-bicycle issue it addresses is rare in our
data -- but removing NMS \emph{and} re-ID together drops the raw
number to 75.4\,\%, a $-7.1$\,pt joint cost, showing the two
fragment-cleanup passes are complementary.

\subsection{Baseline comparison}
For calibration: random uniform across 6 classes is 16.7\,\%,
``detector-only with no motion classifier'' (always
\texttt{uncertain}) is 29.4\,\%, and the majority-class baseline
(always \texttt{approaching}) is 41.2\,\%. The full system more
than doubles the strongest trivial baseline.

\subsection{Failure analysis}
Fig.~\ref{fig:failure} shows the four remaining errors:
trajectory-ambiguity cases where the reversal rule's thresholds
($n\ge6$, $\rho>1.5$, $r_{1/2}<-0.20$) fail to fire (a,b -- a
depth signal would resolve both), and 1-frame parked-bicycle
detections where the classifier correctly abstains because
parked-vs-moving cannot be told from one observation (c,d).
Crucially, \emph{none} falls in a safety-critical confusion
(no \texttt{approaching} $\leftrightarrow$ \texttt{moving\_away}
mix-ups).

\subsection{Negative results we kept honest}
\textbf{ByteTrack on low-fps offline footage} (32.8\,\% even
tuned). Kalman-gated association assumes near-continuous detections;
on 2\,fps footage, predicted positions over $\sim$500\,ms gaps
rarely match observed ones and short detections never ``activate.''
A strong online tracker does not transfer to an offline low-fps
regime where its core motion-continuity assumption is violated.

\textbf{Aggressive stitching breaks established tracks} (75.4\,\%
$\to$ 68.8\,\%). An early re-ID variant allowed merging when
\emph{either} track had $\le 4$ frames; this absorbed 2-frame
tracks (already correctly classified by the salvage rules) into
1-frame fragments, breaking the salvage call. Restricting to
``\emph{both} tracks must be 1 frame'' kept the wins on truly
fragmented fast-crossing objects without regressing any
multi-frame track.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{Approaching has the right asymmetry for a safety alert.}
At precision 0.95 / recall 0.88 (raw) and 1.00 / 0.95
(consolidated), the alert layer's \texttt{APPROACHING} banner is
nearly always correct and rarely misses a true approacher. False
alarms are recoverable; missed approachers are not.

\textbf{Calibrated abstention is auditable.} All 15 evaluated
\texttt{uncertain} labels are predicted \texttt{uncertain}; the
sub-reason (\texttt{short\_track}, \texttt{low\_signal},
\texttt{near\_approaching}, \dots) is quoted in the evidence
string so a pipeline log explains \emph{why} the system declined.
The 3 false-positive \texttt{uncertain} predictions (precision
0.62 raw) correspond exactly to the failure-figure panels and
are the right kind of error -- the system stays silent rather
than lying about intent.

\textbf{On the consolidation decision.} We publish the consolidation
explicitly rather than quietly reporting only the larger number. The
labeler's \texttt{notes} flagged 13 entries as ``fragmentation''/
``duplicate''; 9 collapse cleanly into existing labeled objects.
The raw 82.5\,\% is the metric to compare against any system without
re-ID; the consolidated 92.2\,\% is the ``per real-world hazard''
read.

% --------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

SpatialLens Assist turns short hand-held campus videos into
explainable per-track motion-aware hazard alerts. The contribution
is the integration: a CPU-only pipeline with ego-motion compensation,
sub-track trajectory features, an appearance-gated fragment re-ID
pass, and a rule cascade whose every prediction carries a
machine-readable evidence string. Failure-driven iteration moved
accuracy from 59.0\,\% to 82.5\,\% (raw) / 92.2\,\% (consolidated)
over four rounds. Isolated ablations attribute the dominant gains
to the appearance re-ID pass and the 2-frame salvage rules, and we
report two negative results (ByteTrack on low-fps footage;
aggressive stitching) alongside the positive ones.

\noindent\textbf{Future work.} (a)~A monocular depth backend
(MiDaS~\cite{midas}) would convert bbox-growth from a noisy proxy
to a calibrated depth signal, addressing the trajectory-reversal
residuals. (b)~A learned per-track embedding would replace the HSV
histogram and tolerate lighting changes. (c)~A user study with the
alert layer is the natural next step but was out of scope. (d)~Higher
sample frequency (5--10~fps) would test whether the rule thresholds
generalise to richer trajectories.
