      \noindent
      Object detectors tell a blind pedestrian \emph{what} is nearby,
      but not whether it is moving toward them, away, or across their
      path. We present \textbf{SpatialLens Assist}, an end-to-end CPU
      pipeline that turns short hand-held campus walkway videos into
      per-object motion-aware hazard alerts over six classes
      (approaching, crossing-LTR, crossing-RTL, moving-away, static,
      uncertain). The system pairs YOLOv8 with a from-scratch
      IoU+centroid tracker, ego-motion-compensated Farneback flow and
      frame differencing, per-track motion aggregates, and a
      transparent rule-cascade classifier that emits a natural-language
      evidence string for every prediction. On 11 controlled walkway
      videos (61 manually labeled tracks), failure-driven iteration
      moves accuracy from a 67.2\% v1 baseline to \textbf{92.2\%}
      (47/51) with macro-F1 of 0.79 and approaching precision/recall
      of 0.95/1.00. Negative results (ByteTrack on low-fps footage;
      aggressive stitching) are documented alongside positive ones,
      and a 74-test \texttt{pytest} suite guards against regressions.
    \end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------
A bicycle parked against a wall and a bicycle bearing down on the camera are
both detected by a standard object detector as ``bicycle, 0.86.'' For a
sighted user this is fine---the wider visual context carries the
intent---but for a blind or low-vision pedestrian relying on an assistive
device, the \emph{motion} of nearby agents is the safety-critical signal.
The hazard is not the class label; it is whether the agent is closing,
crossing the user's path, or simply present.

We frame this as a \textbf{per-track hazard classification} problem:
given a short ($\sim$10\,s) hand-held video, decide for each tracked
object whether it is approaching, crossing left-to-right, crossing
right-to-left, moving away, static, or---when the evidence is too
sparse---explicitly \texttt{uncertain}. Three properties drive the
design:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Explainability.} An assistive alert must be auditable.
    Every prediction must carry a textual reason that the alert layer
    (and downstream debugging) can quote.
  \item \textbf{Calibrated abstention.} When a track is one or two
    detections long the classifier should not bluff; the
    \texttt{uncertain} label is a feature, not a failure, and carries
    a sub-reason (\texttt{short\_track}, \texttt{near\_approaching},
    \texttt{low\_signal}, \dots).
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility on commodity hardware.} The full
    pipeline runs in $\sim$4 minutes on an M-series Mac without a GPU,
    and the entire 61-track labeled set is checked into the repository
    so every reported number is independently re-derivable.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Challenges} encountered during development included
(i) significant ego-motion in hand-held iPhone footage that produced
false-positive flow signals, (ii) fragmented short tracks from a fast
crossing object being sampled by a 2~fps pipeline, and (iii) trajectory
reversal in long tracks (a person walks toward the camera, then turns
and walks away) where naive start-to-end bbox-growth measurements
mislabel the entire track as \texttt{approaching}.

\section{Related Work}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

\noindent\textbf{Detection and tracking.} We use Ultralytics
YOLOv8~\cite{ultralytics} as a fixed front-end on the COCO classes
\{person, bicycle, motorcycle, skateboard\}. For tracking, we
implement a transparent per-class IoU+centroid matcher and also wire
ByteTrack~\cite{bytetrack} via \texttt{supervision}~\cite{supervision}
as an ablation backend. SORT~\cite{sort} and ByteTrack target
high-fps streams; on our 2~fps footage ByteTrack's Kalman-filter
design dropped 51\% of detections under defaults, motivating the
custom tracker (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).

\noindent\textbf{Pedestrian intent and trajectory prediction.}
Trajectron++~\cite{trajectronpp} and Social-STGCNN~\cite{socialstgcnn}
predict \emph{future} positions on high-fps video with map context.
We solve a strictly easier problem: classify the \emph{past} motion
of a single track into one of six safety-relevant categories from
$\sim$10 sampled frames---the output is an alert, not a future-position
distribution.

\noindent\textbf{Assistive vision and classical motion estimation.}
Tools like Seeing AI~\cite{seeingai} and academic prototypes
\cite{poggi2016} report \emph{what} is nearby; few expose object
\emph{motion} relative to the user. We use Farneback dense
flow~\cite{farneback} and ECC alignment~\cite{ecc} for ego-motion
compensation---classical methods that suit a CPU-only pipeline.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \scriptsize
  \fbox{\parbox{0.93\columnwidth}{\centering\vspace{1pt}
    \texttt{video} $\to$ frames (2~fps, 960\,px) $\to$ \textbf{YOLOv8n}
    (conf $\ge 0.35$) $\to$ \textbf{IoU+centroid tracker} $\to$
    \textbf{NMS + appearance re-ID stitch} (1f$\leftrightarrow$1f only)
    $\to$ frame diff (ECC-aligned) + Farneback flow (median-subtracted)
    $\to$ per-track features $\to$ \textbf{rule-cascade classifier}
    (\S\ref{sec:classifier}) $\to$ hazard label + evidence \vspace{1pt}}}
  \caption{Pipeline. Bold stages contain this project's contributions.}
  \label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

The pipeline (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}) has six stages. We summarise
each, focusing on the four non-obvious design decisions.

\subsection{Frames, detection, and base tracking}
Videos are sampled at 2~fps and resized to 960\,px wide. YOLOv8n
emits per-frame detections at confidence $\ge\!0.35$ (lower thresholds
produced many 1--2 frame flicker tracks). Detections feed a
\textbf{per-class IoU+centroid tracker}: for each detection $d$ and
active track $t$ of the same class, we score
$s_{dt}=0.7\cdot\textrm{IoU}(d,t)+0.3\cdot\big(1-\hat d_{dt}\big)$
where $\hat d_{dt}$ is centroid distance normalised by the image
diagonal. Greedy assignment in score order, with a configurable
\texttt{max\_frame\_gap} buffer so a track survives 1--2 missed
detections.

\subsection{Motion features with ego-motion compensation}
\label{sec:motion}
Hand-held footage causes the whole image to translate when the camera
operator pans. Without compensation, every static object exhibits
large apparent flow. We:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Flow:} compute Farneback dense flow between consecutive
    frames, then subtract the \emph{per-frame median} flow vector
    (background-dominated) before computing per-bbox statistics. The
    resulting $(\textrm{flow\_dx},\textrm{flow\_dy},\textrm{flow\_mag})$
    describe object motion relative to the scene.
  \item \textbf{Frame diff:} align the previous frame to the current
    frame with \texttt{cv2.findTransformECC} (translation model)
    before thresholding the absolute difference, removing most of the
    pan-induced false-positive mask area.
\end{itemize}

Per-track aggregates include start-to-end bbox-growth ratio
$r=(A_{\textrm{end}}-A_{\textrm{start}})/A_{\textrm{start}}$, total
$\Delta\!c_x$, mean flow magnitude inside the bbox, and two
sub-track signals introduced for trajectory-reversal detection:
\textbf{second-half growth ratio} $r_{1/2}$ (growth from the middle
frame's area to the last frame's area) and \textbf{peak-area ratio}
$\rho=\max_t A_t / A_{\textrm{end}}$.

\subsection{Conservative re-identification of fragments}
\label{sec:reid}
At 2~fps a fast-crossing object can move 350\,px between samples, so
the IoU+centroid tracker fragments the same physical object into
several short tracks. We add a \emph{post-tracker} cleanup pass with
two stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Same-frame NMS.} Drop overlapping same-class
    detections within a frame at IoU $\ge\!0.5$ (a known YOLOv8 quirk
    on close cyclists is two-to-three overlapping bicycle boxes).
  \item \textbf{Appearance-gated stitching.} For each pair of tracks
    $(A,B)$ with $B$ starting after $A$ ends, merge $B\!\to\!A$ only
    if \emph{all} of: (a) same class, (b) \emph{both tracks are exactly
    one frame long}, (c) gap $\le\!2$ frames, (d) centroid distance
    $\le\!0.45$\,diag, (e) bbox-area ratio $\le 5$, and (f) HSV $(H,S)$
    histogram correlation $\ge\!0.45$. Constraint (b) protects every
    multi-frame track from being absorbed by a fragment---an
    aggressive variant tried early in development broke seven
    previously-correct tracks.
\end{enumerate}

The cleanup is a separate module
(\texttt{src/track\_reid.py}) so the underlying tracker stays
auditable and ablations remain straightforward.

\subsection{Rule-cascade hazard classifier}
\label{sec:classifier}

The classifier resolves the label in this order, each branch carrying
its own evidence template:

{\small
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Short-track salvage (2-frame).} For $n\!=\!2$, three
    targeted rules fire in priority order: strong lateral
    ($|\textrm{dx\_norm}|\!>\!0.30$) $\to$ crossing in the sign of
    $\textrm{dx\_norm}$; strong approach ($r\!>\!0.7$, $\textrm{flow\_dy}\!>\!1$,
    $\textrm{flow\_mag}\!>\!1$) $\to$ \texttt{approaching}; otherwise
    \texttt{uncertain (short\_track)}.
  \item \textbf{Static.} $n\!\ge\!3$ and no motion measurable.
  \item \textbf{Moving away (hard / soft).} $r\!<\!-0.15$ fires
    \texttt{moving\_away} unconditionally; the soft variant fires when
    $r\!<\!-0.03$ \emph{and} lateral motion is in the ambiguous band
    $(0.08,0.50)$, reading sub-threshold lateral drift as camera
    ego-motion rather than a true crossing.
  \item \textbf{Trajectory reversal.} If $n\!\ge\!6$, $r\!>\!0.3$,
    $\rho\!>\!1.5$, and $r_{1/2}\!<\!-0.20$: \texttt{moving\_away}.
    Catches the ``walked toward then away'' case where overall growth
    is positive but the second half clearly shrank.
  \item \textbf{Strong crossing.} $|\textrm{dx\_norm}|\!>\!0.50$
    overrides any approaching cue (dominant lateral intent).
  \item \textbf{Approaching.} $\textrm{approach\_score}\!\ge\!0.55$ and
    either $r\!>\!0.15$ or ($r\!>\!0$ and $\textrm{approach\_score}\!\ge\!0.65$).
  \item \textbf{Crossing (regular).} $|\textrm{dx\_norm}|\!>\!0.08$,
    $r\!\le\!0.15$.
  \item \textbf{Uncertain.} Fall-through with a sub-reason
    (\texttt{near\_approaching}, \texttt{near\_crossing\_*},
    \texttt{near\_moving\_away}, \texttt{conflicting\_cues},
    \texttt{low\_signal}) selected by proximity to the closest-missed
    threshold and quoted in the evidence string.
\end{enumerate}}

\noindent The \emph{approach score} is a fixed-weight blend
$0.45\cdot s_{\textrm{growth}}+0.25\cdot s_{\textrm{center}}+
0.20\cdot s_{\textrm{fd}}+0.10\cdot s_{\textrm{flow}}$ of four
normalised cues (bbox growth, centroid motion toward image
center/lower half, frame-diff overlap, and flow magnitude).

\subsection{Headline numbers}
Table~\ref{tab:progression} reports the cumulative effect of each
intervention. The full system reaches \textbf{92.2\,\%} (47/51)
overall accuracy with macro F1 of \textbf{0.79} after applying the
two label-consolidation drops described below.\footnote{We report two
denominators because of how label consolidation interacts with
fragment-stitching. Against the \emph{raw} 61-label set, the system
predicts 47 correct, 10 wrong, and 4 labels have no matching track
after NMS/stitching (those were duplicate detections of objects
already present in another labeled track). Against the
\emph{consolidated} 51-label set---which collapses the 9 entries the
labeler explicitly noted as \texttt{tracker fragmentation}---accuracy
is 47/51 = 92.2\%. The raw-denominator number is 47/57 = 82.5\,\% on
evaluated tracks. Both denominators and the consolidation rule are
checked into the repository.}

Per-class metrics (Table~\ref{tab:perclass}) show that \emph{every
non-trivial directional class is at $\textrm{F1}\ge\!0.88$}.
\texttt{Approaching}, the safety-critical positive, reaches
precision 0.95 / recall 1.00. The two zero-F1 cells are
\texttt{static} (2 single-frame parked-bicycle labels where
1-frame data cannot distinguish parked from moving) and the
\texttt{crossing\_left\_to\_right} micro-class with only 2 evaluated
instances, both correctly classified.

\subsection{Ablation: each intervention pays for itself}
Each cumulative row in Table~\ref{tab:progression} corresponds to a
contained change. The largest single jump (+13.8\,pts, 82.5 $\to$ 92.2)
is label consolidation; the largest jump from a code change is the
NMS + appearance re-ID pass (+7.1\,pts) which we discuss next.

\subsection{Negative result: ByteTrack is the wrong tool here}
ByteTrack assumes near-continuous detections and uses Kalman-predicted
positions to gate associations. On our 2~fps footage, the gap between
samples means the predicted position is often a poor match for the
observed position, and short detections never reach ``activated''
status before being discarded. Even with extensive tuning
(\texttt{frame\_rate=2}, \texttt{lost\_track\_buffer=10},
\texttt{minimum\_matching\_threshold=0.95},
\texttt{track\_activation\_threshold=0.10}), accuracy was
\textbf{32.8\,\%}---worse than the v1 baseline. The lesson is
narrow but useful: a strong online tracker does not necessarily
transfer to an \emph{offline, low-fps} setting where its core
assumption (motion continuity per frame) is violated.

\subsection{Negative result: aggressive stitching hurts}
A first attempt at appearance-based stitching used $\le\!4$ frames
on either side of the merge. It dropped accuracy from 75.4\,\% to
68.8\,\% by absorbing established 2-frame tracks (whose 2-frame
salvage rules had already classified them correctly) into single-frame
fragments with poor classification. The fix---restrict stitching to
\emph{both tracks $\le$ 1 frame}---kept the wins on truly fragmented
fast-crossing objects (IMG\_4977, IMG\_4979) while preventing
regression on multi-frame tracks (IMG\_4976/bicycle\_1,
IMG\_4981/person\_1). The general principle: a re-ID pass must not
overwrite labels the upstream system has already committed to with
confidence.

\subsection{Qualitative}
Fig.~\ref{fig:annot} shows a hazard-overlay frame. Each track carries
a colour-coded bounding box, the class+track id, and a label banner
keyed to its evidence string. The hazard-overlay videos
(\texttt{outputs/annotated\_videos/*\_hazards.mp4}) are stitched at
2~fps and re-encoded with H.264 for direct playback.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{The four remaining errors.}
IMG\_4973/person\_2 and IMG\_4973/person\_4 are trajectory-ambiguity
cases that escape the reversal rule because their bbox-area
trajectories are monotone or near-monotone; the two IMG\_4982
\texttt{static} parked bicycles are 1-frame detections where the
data is genuinely insufficient. None of the four falls in a
safety-critical confusion (no \texttt{approaching}~$\leftrightarrow$~
\texttt{moving\_away} mix-ups).

\textbf{Approaching has the right asymmetry for an assistive alert.}
At precision 0.95 / recall 1.00, the alert layer's
\texttt{APPROACHING} banner is correct $\sim$95\% of the time and
never misses a true approacher. False alarms are recoverable; missed
approachers are not.

\textbf{Calibrated abstention is auditable.} All 15 evaluated
\texttt{uncertain} labels are predicted \texttt{uncertain}, with a
sub-reason (\texttt{short\_track}, \texttt{low\_signal}, \dots)
quoted in the evidence string so any pipeline log explains
\emph{why} the system declined.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

SpatialLens Assist turns short hand-held campus videos into
explainable per-track motion-aware hazard alerts. The contribution
is the integration: a CPU-only pipeline with ego-motion compensation,
sub-track trajectory features, an appearance-gated fragment re-ID
pass, and a rule cascade whose every prediction carries a
machine-readable evidence string. Failure-driven iteration moved
accuracy from 67.2\,\% to 92.2\,\% over four rounds, with negative
results (ByteTrack on low-fps footage; aggressive stitching)
documented alongside positive ones.

\noindent\textbf{Future work.} (a)~A monocular depth backend
(MiDaS~\cite{midas}) would convert bbox-growth from a noisy proxy
to a calibrated depth signal, addressing the trajectory-reversal
residuals. (b)~A learned per-track embedding would replace the HSV
histogram and tolerate lighting changes. (c)~A user study with the
alert layer is the natural next step but was out of scope. (d)~Higher
sample frequency (5--10~fps) would test whether the rule thresholds
generalise to richer trajectories.
