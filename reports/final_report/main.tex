\section{Introduction}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------
A bicycle parked against a wall and a bicycle bearing down on the camera
are both detected by a standard detector as ``bicycle, 0.86.'' For a
sighted user the wider visual context carries the intent; for a blind
or low-vision pedestrian relying on an assistive device, that wider
context is exactly what is missing. The safety-critical signal is not
the class label but the \emph{motion}: whether the agent is closing,
crossing the user's path, or merely present.

\noindent\textbf{Research question.}
SpatialLens Assist asks whether lightweight, interpretable motion
cues are sufficient to classify pedestrian-scale hazards in low-fps
egocentric campus video. In contrast to learned trajectory
classifiers that require larger labelled datasets, we use a
transparent rule cascade over ego-motion-compensated optical flow,
centroid displacement, and bounding-box growth. Rather than training
a learned trajectory classifier on a small dataset, SpatialLens
evaluates whether a transparent rule cascade can achieve high
accuracy on decidable tracks while abstaining when temporal evidence
is insufficient.

\noindent\textbf{Approach in one paragraph.}
We frame motion-aware hazard detection as a \emph{per-track
classification} problem: given a $\sim$10\,s phone clip sampled at
2\,fps, decide for each tracked object whether it is approaching,
crossing left-to-right, crossing right-to-left, moving away, static,
or---when temporal evidence is too sparse---explicitly
\texttt{uncertain}. Three design properties drive the system:
(i)~\textbf{explainability}---every prediction carries a textual
reason the alert layer can quote;
(ii)~\textbf{calibrated abstention}---short tracks should not bluff,
so \texttt{uncertain} is a first-class output with a sub-reason
(\texttt{short\_track}, \texttt{low\_signal}, \dots); and
(iii)~\textbf{reproducibility}---the pipeline runs in $\sim$4\,min on
a GPU-less M-series Mac, and the 61-track labelled set is committed.

\noindent\textbf{Summary of findings.}
On 11 controlled campus walkway videos with 61 manually-labelled
tracks, the system reaches 82.5\% accuracy on 57 matched tracks and
93.3\% on the 30 tracks with at least three frames; the safety-critical
\texttt{approaching} class reaches 0.95\,/\,0.88
precision/recall; \emph{no} \texttt{approaching} $\leftrightarrow$
\texttt{moving\_away} confusion is observed. Ablations attribute the
dominant accuracy gain to track continuity rather than classifier
complexity: appearance-gated fragment re-identification
contributes 6.2\,pts and the two 2-frame salvage rules contribute
3.5\,pts each, while ByteTrack on this 2\,fps offline footage drops
to 32.8\%---a useful negative result.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*.}, leftmargin=*]
  \item A complete offline egocentric video pipeline for motion-aware
        campus hazard alerts, evaluable end-to-end on CPU in
        $\sim$4~minutes.
  \item Ego-motion-compensated motion features (Farneback flow with
        per-frame median subtraction; ECC-aligned frame differencing)
        appropriate to handheld low-fps footage.
  \item An interpretable hazard rule cascade with natural-language
        evidence strings and conservative abstention, including a
        trajectory-reversal heuristic for ``approached-then-turned-away''
        tracks.
  \item A manually-labelled 61-track evaluation set across 11 campus
        videos, with two reporting denominators (raw and per-physical-object
        consolidation) and a fully scripted ablation suite.
  \item Empirical evidence that on this regime tracking continuity
        dominates classifier sophistication: appearance-gated re-ID
        and short-track salvage are the largest gains, and ByteTrack
        is poorly matched to sparse offline footage.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

\noindent\textbf{Object detection and tracking for assistive
navigation.}
YOLOv8~\cite{ultralytics} is the fixed COCO-class detector front-end
used here; trackers such as SORT~\cite{sort} and
ByteTrack~\cite{bytetrack} are strong online baselines but assume
near-continuous per-frame detections from a Kalman model. On our
2\,fps offline footage ByteTrack (via
\texttt{supervision}~\cite{supervision}) drops 51\% of detections
under defaults---a known limitation of motion-continuity assumptions
in sparse sampling regimes~\cite{bytetrack}. We therefore implement
a transparent per-class IoU+centroid matcher with an explicit gap
buffer and a separate appearance-gated fragment re-ID pass tailored
to the sparse-sampling regime. Assistive vision tools such as
Seeing AI~\cite{seeingai} and academic prototypes~\cite{poggi2016}
expose object \emph{identity} but generally not \emph{motion intent}
relative to the user; their alerts read ``person'' or ``bicycle,''
not ``bike crossing right.''

\noindent\textbf{Optical flow and ego-motion for motion reasoning.}
Classical Farneback flow~\cite{farneback} and ECC
alignment~\cite{ecc} are well suited to the CPU-only constraint of
an offline assistive prototype. We use both, and explicitly subtract
the per-frame median flow vector to compensate for the camera pan
that dominates handheld footage---without this step, every static
object exhibits large apparent motion. The same idea has been used
in classical SLAM and recent learned ego-motion
estimation~\cite{midas}; we deliberately stay in the classical
regime to keep the pipeline GPU-free and interpretable.

\noindent\textbf{Explainable rule-based vs.\ learned trajectory
classifiers.}
Modern trajectory forecasters such as
Trajectron++~\cite{trajectronpp} and
Social-STGCNN~\cite{socialstgcnn} predict \emph{future} positions
over 4--8\,s from high-fps tracks with map context and require
thousands of labelled trajectories---more data than this project
collects, and an output format (continuous future positions)
mismatched to a spoken hazard alert. We solve the strictly easier
problem of classifying the \emph{past} motion of a single track into
six safety-relevant categories from $\sim$10 sampled frames, and we
choose to output an alert with an evidence string rather than a
future-position distribution. This is a deliberate scope decision
driven by (i)~the no-training-data setting, (ii)~the explainability
requirement of an assistive interface, and (iii)~the conservative
abstention requirement when temporal evidence is sparse.

\noindent\textbf{Why low-fps egocentric footage is challenging.}
At 2\,fps a fast-crossing object can move $>$300\,px between samples;
Kalman trackers lose the gate, IoU trackers cannot match the new
detection to the prior, and naive bbox-growth features confound
camera pan with object motion. These three failure modes motivate
the three corresponding design choices in
\Cref{sec:method}: an explicit gap buffer, a conservative
post-tracker re-ID pass, and per-frame ego-motion subtraction.

The pipeline (\Cref{fig:pipeline}) has six stages. We summarise each,
focusing on the four non-obvious design decisions.

\subsection{Frames, detection, and base tracking}
Videos are sampled at 2\,fps and resized to 960\,px wide. YOLOv8n
emits per-frame detections at confidence $\ge\!0.35$ (lower thresholds
produced many 1--2 frame flicker tracks). Detections feed a
\textbf{per-class IoU+centroid tracker}: for each detection $d$ and
active track $t$ of the same class, we score
$s_{dt}=0.7\cdot\textrm{IoU}(d,t)+0.3\cdot\big(1-\hat d_{dt}\big)$,
where $\hat d_{dt}$ is centroid distance normalised by the image
diagonal. Greedy assignment in score order, with a configurable
\texttt{max\_frame\_gap} buffer so a track survives 1--2 missed
detections.

\subsection{Motion features with ego-motion compensation}
\label{sec:motion}
Handheld footage translates the whole image during a pan, so every
static object shows large apparent flow. We compensate two ways.
\textbf{Flow:} Farneback dense flow between frames, minus the
\emph{per-frame median} flow vector (background-dominated), giving
per-bbox $(\textrm{flow\_dx},\textrm{flow\_dy},\textrm{flow\_mag})$
relative to the scene. \textbf{Frame diff:} we align the previous frame
to the current with \texttt{cv2.findTransformECC} (translation) before
differencing, removing most pan-induced false-positive mask area.

Per-track aggregates include the start-to-end bbox-growth ratio
$r=(A_{\textrm{end}}-A_{\textrm{start}})/A_{\textrm{start}}$, total
$\Delta\!c_x$, mean flow magnitude inside the bbox, and two sub-track
signals introduced for trajectory-reversal detection:
\textbf{second-half growth ratio} $r_{1/2}$ (growth from the middle
frame's area to the last frame's area) and \textbf{peak-area ratio}
$\rho=\max_t A_t / A_{\textrm{end}}$.

\subsection{Conservative re-identification of fragments}
\label{sec:reid}
At 2\,fps a fast-crossing object can move 350\,px between samples,
so the IoU+centroid tracker fragments the same physical object into
several short tracks. We add a \emph{post-tracker} cleanup pass with
two stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Same-frame NMS.} Drop overlapping same-class
    detections within a frame at IoU $\ge\!0.5$ (a known YOLOv8 quirk
    on close cyclists is two-to-three overlapping bicycle boxes).
  \item \textbf{Appearance-gated stitching.} For each pair of tracks
    $(A,B)$ with $B$ starting after $A$ ends, merge $B\!\to\!A$ only
    if \emph{all} of: (a) same class, (b) \emph{both tracks are exactly
    one frame long}, (c) gap $\le\!2$ frames, (d) centroid distance
    $\le\!0.45$\,diag, (e) bbox-area ratio $\le\!5$, and (f) HSV $(H,S)$
    histogram correlation $\ge\!0.45$. Constraint (b) protects every
    multi-frame track from being absorbed by a fragment---an
    aggressive variant tried early in development broke seven
    previously-correct tracks (\Cref{sec:bytetrack}).
\end{enumerate}

The cleanup is a separate module (\texttt{src/track\_reid.py}) so the
tracker stays auditable and ablations remain straightforward.

\subsection{Rule-cascade hazard classifier}
\label{sec:classifier}

The classifier resolves the label by a priority cascade, each branch
carrying its own evidence template ($r$: bbox-growth ratio;
$\rho$: peak/end area ratio; $r_{1/2}$: second-half growth):
\textbf{(1)~Short-track salvage} ($n\!=\!2$): strong lateral
$|\textrm{dx\_norm}|\!>\!0.30\to$crossing, else strong approach
($r\!>\!0.7$, downward flow)$\to$\texttt{approaching}, else
\texttt{uncertain}. \textbf{(2)~Static} if $n\!\ge\!3$ and no motion.
\textbf{(3)~Moving-away} if $r\!<\!-0.15$, or the \emph{soft} variant
($-0.15\!<\!r\!<\!-0.03$ with sub-strong lateral motion read as
ego-motion drift). \textbf{(4)~Trajectory reversal} ($n\!\ge\!6$,
$r\!>\!0.3$, $\rho\!>\!1.5$, $r_{1/2}\!<\!-0.20$)$\to$
\texttt{moving\_away} for the ``approached then turned away'' case.
\textbf{(5)~Strong crossing} ($|\textrm{dx\_norm}|\!>\!0.50$)
overrides approach. \textbf{(6)~Approaching} if
$\textrm{approach\_score}\!\ge\!0.55$ and ($r\!>\!0.15$ or the
slow-approach allowance $r\!>\!0$ with score $\ge\!0.65$).
\textbf{(7)~Crossing} if $|\textrm{dx\_norm}|\!>\!0.08$, $r\!\le\!0.15$.
\textbf{(8)~Uncertain} fall-through, tagged with the closest-missed
sub-reason (\texttt{near\_approaching}, \texttt{low\_signal}, \dots)
quoted in the evidence string.

\noindent The \emph{approach score} is a fixed-weight blend
$0.45\cdot s_{\textrm{growth}}+0.25\cdot s_{\textrm{center}}+
0.20\cdot s_{\textrm{fd}}+0.10\cdot s_{\textrm{flow}}$ of four
normalised cues (bbox growth, centroid motion toward image
center/lower half, frame-diff overlap, and flow magnitude).

\subsection{Alert generation and explainability}
Every prediction is paired with a templated \emph{evidence string}
quoting the specific feature values that triggered the cascade
branch (e.g.\ ``\texttt{bicycle\_1 classified as approaching because
bbox grew by 11.42 and approach\_score = 1.00; centroid moved toward
image center/lower half; flow\_mag = 17.26}''). For abstentions the
evidence string names the sub-reason: ``\texttt{classified as
uncertain (short\_track) because track is only 2 frames long
(min = 3), so trajectory and bbox-growth cues are unreliable}.''
Because the application is assistive navigation, unsupported motion
claims are undesirable: we therefore treat uncertainty as a
safety-preserving abstention---tracks with fewer than three frames
are not forced into a motion class.

\Cref{fig:temporal} visualises the mechanism on a representative
\texttt{approaching} track: across four sampled frames of a
15-frame cyclist, the bounding box grows by $21.98\times$, the
yellow centroid trail moves toward the image centre, and the
approach score saturates at 1.00, yielding an unambiguous
\texttt{approaching} call.

\subsection{Videos and tracks}
We recorded 11 controlled $\sim$10\,s phone videos on Stanford
walkways under daylight, sampling at 2\,fps to 960\,px-wide frames
(\Cref{tab:dataset}). Each captures a single scripted hazard mode
(bike approaching, pedestrian crossing, etc.) so that label intent
is unambiguous. Two additional natural-walk clips are used for
qualitative demonstration only and are excluded from the
quantitative tables.

\subsection{Track-level labels and class distribution}
Every detected, tracked object in the 11 controlled videos was
labelled by hand into the six-class taxonomy described in the
Introduction (\Cref{tab:classes}). The six classes are
deliberately motion-centric: \texttt{approaching}, \texttt{moving\_away},
\texttt{crossing\_left\_to\_right}, \texttt{crossing\_right\_to\_left},
\texttt{static}, and \texttt{uncertain}. The class distribution is
imbalanced---\texttt{approaching} and \texttt{uncertain} dominate,
while \texttt{static} has only two examples (both short tracker
fragments of parked bicycles). This imbalance is realistic for
campus walkway footage but informs how the per-class numbers should
be read (\Cref{sec:results,sec:failure}).

\subsection{Track--label matching and evaluated denominator}
Predictions and labels are matched by \texttt{(video\_id,
track\_id)} pairs. After post-tracker re-identification, 4 of the
61 labelled tracks were absorbed by NMS/re-ID into an existing
labelled track (their detections moved into a different
\texttt{track\_id}), so 57 prediction--label pairs remain for the
raw-denominator evaluation. We also publish a 51-track
\emph{consolidated} label set in which the labeller's
\texttt{notes} column marked 9 entries as ``fragmentation'' or
``duplicate''; these collapse cleanly into one representative each.
The raw 82.5\% is the metric to compare against any system without
re-ID; the consolidated 92.2\% reads as accuracy \emph{per unique
physical object}. Both label sets are versioned in the repository.

\subsection{Metrics}
We report overall accuracy on the 57 evaluated tracks; the
\emph{decidable} accuracy on the 30 tracks with at least 3 frames
(the regime in which the cascade has enough temporal evidence to
classify motion); per-class precision/recall/F1 and macro-F1; the
full $6\times 6$ confusion matrix; and an ablation suite that
toggles individual contributions one at a time. We use
sklearn-compatible support counts, and we report two reporting
denominators (raw and consolidated) rather than picking the
flattering one. Macro-F1 is lower than accuracy because rare classes
such as \texttt{static} and \texttt{crossing\_RTL} have small
support; macro-F1 weights every class equally, while accuracy is
dominated by \texttt{approaching} and \texttt{uncertain} tracks.

\Cref{tab:perclass} reports the per-class metrics. The
safety-critical \texttt{approaching} class reaches
\textbf{0.95\,/\,0.88} precision/recall---an asymmetry an alert layer
wants, since false alarms are recoverable but missed approachers are
not. Every non-trivial directional class has precision 1.00, so when
the cascade commits to a directional label it is essentially never
wrong; what it does instead, when temporal evidence is thin, is
abstain into \texttt{uncertain}. The gap between overall accuracy
(82.5\%) and decidable accuracy (93.3\%) is explained primarily by
short tracker fragments: when at least three frames are available
the rule cascade has enough temporal evidence to classify motion
reliably; when only one or two detections are linked, the system
usually abstains.

Results are not single-video luck. 6 of the 11 videos are
classified perfectly, and per-video accuracy ranges from 1/3
(IMG\_4977, the densest fast-crossing fragmentation) to 16/16; the
worst absolute count is IMG\_4982 (11/16 raw) where overlapping
pedestrians and parked bicycles fragment into 1-frame tracks the
re-ID gate is too conservative to merge.

\subsection{Motion feature space}
\Cref{fig:scatter} plots every labelled track in the
$(dx_\textrm{norm},\, r)$ feature space the cascade uses, coloured by
ground truth. \texttt{approaching} tracks (red) cluster in the
positive-growth region; \texttt{moving\_away} tracks (green)
cluster in the negative-growth region; \texttt{crossing} tracks
(blue/purple) sit at large $|dx_\textrm{norm}|$; and
\texttt{uncertain} tracks (orange) cluster near the origin, where
no decisive cue is available. The classifier's dashed thresholds
($|dx_\textrm{norm}|\!\in\!\{0.08, 0.50\}$ and $r\!\in\!\{-0.15,
0.15\}$) sit exactly between the class clusters---a visual confirmation
that two features carry most of the decision boundary.

\subsection{Ablation study}
\label{sec:ablations}
\Cref{fig:ablations} and \Cref{tab:ablations} show what happens when
each contribution is removed one at a time and the full pipeline is
re-run. The largest improvement comes from track continuity rather
than classifier complexity: appearance-gated re-ID stitching is the
single largest contributor ($-6.2$\,pts raw when removed), and the
two 2-frame salvage rules each contribute $\sim$3.5\,pts. Toggling
\emph{both} NMS and re-ID off together drops the raw number to
75.4\%, a $-7.0$\,pt joint cost, showing the two fragment-cleanup
passes are complementary. The classifier-side rules (trajectory
reversal, soft moving-away, slow-approach allowance) contribute
$\sim$1.8\,pts each---meaningful but not dominant. \emph{Same-frame
NMS alone barely moves the raw metric} because the overlapping-bicycle
issue it addresses is rare in our data, but it is essential to keep
the bookkeeping clean for downstream re-ID.

\subsection{Baseline calibration and ByteTrack negative result}
\label{sec:bytetrack}
For calibration: random uniform across 6 classes is 16.7\%,
``detector-only with no motion classifier'' (always
\texttt{uncertain}) is 29.4\%, and the majority-class baseline
(always \texttt{approaching}) is 41.2\%. The full system more
than doubles the strongest trivial baseline.

\textbf{ByteTrack on low-fps offline footage} achieves only 32.8\%
even after tuning. Kalman-gated association assumes near-continuous
detections; on 2\,fps footage, predicted positions over $\sim$500\,ms
gaps rarely match observed ones, and short detections never
``activate'' a track. ByteTrack is a useful negative result:
although it is strong for many multi-object tracking settings, its
assumptions are poorly matched to sparse 2\,fps offline footage---
in our clips it drops many low-confidence detections, reducing the
temporal evidence available to the downstream classifier.

\textbf{Aggressive stitching breaks established tracks} (75.4\%
$\to$ 68.8\%). An early re-ID variant allowed merging when
\emph{either} track had $\le 4$ frames; this absorbed 2-frame tracks
(already correctly classified by the salvage rules) into 1-frame
fragments, breaking the salvage call. Restricting to ``\emph{both}
tracks must be 1 frame'' kept the wins on truly fragmented
fast-crossing objects without regressing any multi-frame track.

\section{Failure Analysis and Limitations}
\label{sec:failure}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Uncertainty breakdown}
\Cref{fig:uncert} shows the sub-reason every \texttt{uncertain}
prediction carries. The distribution is heavily concentrated on
\texttt{short\_track} (24-frame budget across all 11 videos): 23 of
the 24 \texttt{uncertain} predictions abstain because the underlying
track has fewer than 3 frames, and only 1 abstains because the
motion signal is too weak to commit on a longer track. This is the
single most important diagnostic in the paper: uncertainty in
SpatialLens is not a classifier failure mode---it is the
tracker-fragmentation tax, made auditable.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{uncertainty_breakdown.pdf}
  \caption{Sub-reason breakdown for all \texttt{uncertain}
  predictions. 23 of 24 abstentions are \texttt{short\_track} (the
  track has $<\!3$ frames); only one is \texttt{low\_signal}.
  Uncertainty in this system is dominated by tracker fragmentation,
  not classifier ambiguity.}
  \label{fig:uncert}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Static-class result}
The static-class F1 of 0.00 should not be interpreted as a general
static-object failure. Only two static examples appear in the
evaluation set, both as one-frame or two-frame tracker fragments
(parked bicycles detected in a single frame). The cascade correctly
abstains rather than committing, which is the safety-preserving
behaviour we want; with the current label set it is impossible to
test whether the \texttt{static} branch ever fires correctly on a
long, stationary track. A larger evaluation set with multi-frame
static examples is the cleanest fix.

\subsection{Error taxonomy}
\Cref{tab:errors} groups the residual errors into five root causes
and lists a concrete example and proposed fix for each. Two
observations matter. First, \emph{the dominant failure is
fragmentation, not misclassification}: short-track fragmentation
and static abstention together account for 5 of the 10 residual
errors. Second, \emph{none of the residual errors are in the
safety-critical asymmetry}: the
\texttt{approaching}$\leftrightarrow$\texttt{moving\_away} confusion
is zero, and the only directional mistake
(\texttt{moving\_away}$\to$\texttt{approaching}, IMG\_4973 /
person\_4) is a monotone-bbox-growth edge case the
trajectory-reversal rule misses ($\rho\!=\!1.17 < 1.5$ threshold).

\subsection{Limitations}
\textbf{Dataset size.} The evaluation set is small (61 labelled
tracks, 11 videos) and class-imbalanced (only 2 \texttt{static}
examples, 3 \texttt{crossing\_RTL}). As a result, macro-F1 is
sensitive to rare-class behaviour, and the reported numbers should
be interpreted as evidence that the proposed motion features are
promising---not as a deployment-level claim.

\textbf{No user study.} We have not yet tested whether the alert
strings are useful to a blind or low-vision pedestrian under real
walking conditions. The 0.95/0.88 \texttt{approaching}
precision/recall is the right asymmetry for a safety alert in
principle; a user-centred study is the appropriate next validation.

\textbf{Low-fps regime.} The pipeline is built for 2\,fps offline
processing. Higher sampling (5--10\,fps) would alleviate most of the
fragmentation residuals (\Cref{fig:uncert}), but the threshold
constants in the cascade would need to be re-tuned because they
scale with inter-frame displacement.

\textbf{No depth.} Bounding-box growth is a noisy proxy for
``getting closer.'' The one residual directional mistake
(\texttt{moving\_away}$\to$\texttt{approaching}) is exactly the
ambiguity a monocular depth backend would resolve.

\textbf{Fixed detector.} We use YOLOv8n's COCO classes unchanged.
Skateboards and scooters are sometimes mis-classed by the detector;
the cascade inherits those errors. A small fine-tune on campus
hazards would help, but it is outside the scope of an
interpretable-classifier study.

% --------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------

SpatialLens Assist shows that interpretable motion cues can produce
useful hazard labels from low-fps egocentric campus video. The
system is strongest when tracks persist for at least three frames,
reaching 93.3\% decidable accuracy, and its primary failure mode is
conservative abstention caused by tracker fragmentation rather than
dangerous mislabelling: the
\texttt{approaching}$\leftrightarrow$\texttt{moving\_away} confusion
is zero, and 23 of 24 abstentions are honest short-track aborts. The
ablation study suggests that for assistive navigation at this
project scale, \emph{improving temporal track continuity matters
more than replacing the interpretable classifier with a more complex
learned model}---appearance-gated re-ID and short-track salvage
together contribute roughly twice as much accuracy as all
classifier-side rule changes combined. Future work targets the four
limitations above: a monocular depth backend (MiDaS~\cite{midas}) to
turn bbox growth from a noisy proxy into a calibrated signal; a
learned per-track embedding to replace the HSV histogram in the
re-ID gate; a denser sampling regime (5--10\,fps); and a
user-centred evaluation of the alert layer with blind and
low-vision walkers.
